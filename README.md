Title: Global CO2 and Greenhouse Gas Emissions Analysis and Prediction

This project developed and evaluated several machine learning models to predict a country's CO2 emissions based on economic and demographic factors, while also exploring global emission trends and patterns.

Global CO2 and greenhouse gas emissions are a critical driver of climate change, with far-reaching consequences for ecosystems, economies, and human societies. Understanding the factors that contribute to these emissions and being able to predict future trends is essential for policymakers, environmental scientists, and international organizations working to mitigate climate change. Without accurate insights, efforts to reduce emissions and adapt to climate impacts can be misdirected or ineffective.

I used the "Our World in Data" CO2 and Greenhouse Gas Emissions dataset, accessed by Collab. After initial loading, the dataset contained over 50,000 rows and 79 columns, capturing annual emissions data for numerous countries and aggregated regions from 1750 to 2022. During exploratory data analysis, it became apparent that many entries represented aggregate regions (like 'World' or 'Europe'), which I later filtered out to focus on individual country-level analysis. I also observed significant missing values, particularly in gdp and population for earlier years, and in CO2 for some entries.

My goal was to predict a country's CO2 emissions. I began by performing extensive data cleaning, including filtering out non-country data and imputing missing gdp and population values using country-specific medians, and setting missing co2 and total_ghg to zero. After cleaning, I conducted an in-depth Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) to understand distributions, identify top emitters, and visualize trends over time and across continents. For modeling, I used five different regression models: Linear Regression, Decision Tree Regressor, Random Forest Regressor, Gradient Boosting Regressor, and K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) Regressor. These models were chosen to explore a range of complexities, from simple linear relationships to more intricate non-linear patterns, and were evaluated using Mean Squared Error (MSE) and R-squared (R2).

My most successful model was the Random Forest Regressor, achieving an R-squared score of approximately 0.9756, indicating it explained about 97.56% of the variance in log-transformed CO2 emissions. This model significantly outperformed the baseline Linear Regression (R2 = 0.6576) and even other ensemble methods, such as Gradient Boosting (R2 = 0.8746). The visualization of actual vs. predicted values for the Random Forest Regressor clearly shows predictions closely hugging the ideal diagonal line, demonstrating its strong predictive power. 

While this project didn't explicitly incorporate demographic subgroups for a fairness check in the context of individual predictions (as the dataset is country-level rather than individual-level), the continental analysis revealed stark disparities. Continents like North America and Asia consistently showed higher average annual CO2 emissions compared to Africa or South America. This suggests that factors like industrialization, economic development, and historical energy consumption patterns create inherent 'unfairness' in global emission burdens, impacting climate change disproportionately. Future work could explore if the models perform differently across countries with varying levels of economic development or historical emissions responsibilities.

One limitation was the strategy for co2 and total_ghg where missing values were set to zero. This is a strong assumption that might not always hold. Another is the use of aggregate country-level data, which doesn't account for intra-country variations. With more time, I would explore more sophisticated imputation techniques, investigate time-series specific models given the nature of the data, and perhaps incorporate additional external features like policy changes or technological advancements. I would also dive into interpretability techniques for the Random Forest model to understand feature importance better.
